3 - DESIGN IDEATION POSTER

Brainstorming session

For this assessment, our task was to explore a range of datasets and brainstorm ideas for a potential final project. Initially, we were assigned to form groups and work with different datasets provided by our instructor. My first group began by examining a dataset on “Real Estate Maps”, but we quickly encountered issues due to its outdated nature. After some discussion, we decided to pivot to a new dataset focused on "Overseas Travel by QLD State Library Staff". This dataset intrigued us because it contained detailed information on staff travel patterns, costs, and sponsorships. We envisioned creating an interactive digital map that would allow users to view travel trends, costs, and destinations over the years.

Later, we had to switch groups and generate a new idea from scratch. In the second group, we focused on creating a "Biodiversity Tracker" which would allow users to share wildlife sightings, with features for predicting future observations. The platform would be particularly useful for environmentalists and educational institutions. Despite the challenges with complex dataset formats, we managed to develop a promising concept before running out of time.

After class, I reflected on the day’s activities and decided to move forward with the idea of a wildlife enthusiast platform. Drawing inspiration from the second group’s brainstorming session, I developed the idea further into a social media platform called "WildSnap". This platform would allow users to upload wildlife pictures, share observation details, and analyze the data to identify trends. The concept was well-received during the ideation session, and feedback suggested adding gamification features to engage users more effectively.

In comparison to other ideas, such as a Bicycle Map for Brisbane and a QLD Hospitals Dataset tool, my WildSnap concept stood out for its combination of data analysis and user engagement, making it a strong contender for the final project.

Poster & Presentation

After refining my idea, I began working on the structure and design for the WildSnap application. The initial steps involved creating UI mockups using Figma to outline the key design elements and layouts for 4-5 pages, focusing on providing a user-friendly and engaging interface. I also utilized Canva to craft the textual content, clearly communicating the app's features, description, and benefits. WildSnap is aimed at wildlife enthusiasts looking to share their sightings and analyze trends. On presentation day, we uploaded our posters to individual Padlets, allowing for interactive feedback and engagement from peers. The positive reception of both the design and concept was encouraging, though seeing other innovative ideas like the Brisbane BBQ Locator highlighted the importance of balancing creativity with practicality.

REFLECTION

Throughout the preparation phase, I initially focused on developing a social media platform for wildlife enthusiasts, WildSnap. However, reflecting on the process and feedback, several key realizations shaped my approach moving forward. Firstly, I recognized the importance of aligning core features with user needs. For instance, while the initial idea of wildlife data analysis was engaging, the suggestion to integrate gamification added significant value by enhancing user interaction and engagement, a feature I hadn't initially considered.

This experience underscored the value of adaptability and proactive feedback integration. The ideation process emphasized the need to balance creativity with practicality, especially when comparing ideas like the Brisbane BBQ Locator or Artworks Locator, which highlighted the importance of user-centric design. Furthermore, working with complex datasets, particularly in my biodiversity tracker brainstorming, reinforced the necessity of robust data handling skills and the validation of ideas early in the process to avoid potential roadblocks.

A critical takeaway from this experience is the importance of continuous refinement. By remaining open to peer suggestions and learning from diverse project concepts, I now realize how valuable feedback can be in driving innovation and ensuring that ideas remain practical and user-focused. This reflection has pushed me to explore gamification in WildSnap and reinforced the need for balancing creativity with real-world application in future projects.

4C - WORK-IN-PROGRESS MVP

In this phase of our project, we built on the feedback we received during earlier discussions and made substantial adjustments to our design and development plan. Our team began with paper prototyping in a studio setting, refining our ideas based on classroom insights and lessons from prior web design projects. We then initiated the process of implementing the core features of our website and ultimately presented our Minimum Viable Product (MVP) to the class.

One of the most significant design decisions we made was simplifying the extensive feature set initially planned. Instead of building out a broad array of functionalities, we focused on delivering a core set of features. This included a search tool for wildlife, an encyclopedia page, and a quiz section, each grounded in using real-world datasets. Through classroom feedback and prototyping evaluations, we realized that narrowing the scope allowed us to focus on creating a more polished and functional MVP.

Another important pivot involved deciding which APIs to integrate. While we initially intended to use multiple APIs, the complexity of their implementation led us to focus on one, ensuring we fully understood how to use it before integrating others. This shift in strategy gave us a clearer path forward and set more achievable goals for our development timeline.

Minimal Viable Product (MVP)

Our MVP included the three core features mentioned earlier: the search tool, encyclopedia page, and quiz section. The search tool allowed users to look up wildlife species, which were displayed using a dataset integrated into the site. The encyclopedia page featured information about wildlife, pulling data from the Wikipedia API. The quiz section was designed to engage users with multiple-choice questions to test their knowledge of various species.

One feature we worked hard to implement was an interactive map, where users could view species’ locations based on datasets. Though functional, the map was relatively basic at this stage, with hardcoded data for demonstration purposes. Our plan was to expand its interactivity by dynamically loading location data in future iterations.

While we successfully delivered a functional MVP, there were some features, like the trip-planning tool, that weren’t part of the initial MVP but were worked on in parallel. This feature, which optimized routes based on user inputs, was an added bonus that we plan to integrate in later versions.

My Contribution

My primary responsibility in this phase was to develop the encyclopedia page and assist with API integration. This involved fetching and displaying data using the Wikipedia API, along with designing an intuitive user interface. I also helped troubleshoot issues with the quiz page and contributed to debugging problems with the dropdown select feature.

Additionally, I developed the trip-planning algorithm, a secondary feature not required for the MVP but which impressed my team. This involved coding an optimal route generator that linked to Google Maps for users to view their planned trips.

Feedback

After presenting our MVP, we received constructive feedback from our tutors and peers. Tutors emphasized the importance of making the interface more user-friendly, particularly suggesting improvements to the quiz and search functionalities. They also noted that we could streamline some interactions, especially in how users navigate between different sections of the website.

During user testing, our MVP was well-received. Testers enjoyed the quiz feature and found the encyclopedia page informative, but they highlighted a few areas where the user experience could be refined. For instance, they recommended adding more visual cues to indicate when users had successfully completed a task and simplifying the navigation between the quiz and encyclopedia sections.

REFLECTION

Reflecting on our MVP presentation, I believe we did a good job of delivering a cohesive product that focused on core features. Our decision to narrow our scope allowed us to create a more polished MVP, which was well-received by our tutors and peers. However, in retrospect, I would have prioritized more rigorous testing of the dropdown select feature and spent additional time ensuring all interactive elements functioned smoothly.

Moreover, this experience highlighted the importance of flexibility in the development process. As we encountered unexpected challenges, such as API integration complexities, we adjusted our approach accordingly. These setbacks taught us to balance ambition with practicality and to ensure our project goals remained realistic within our timeline.

My Contributions

Over the last several weeks, my primary responsibility has been developing the Trip Planning feature for our product, an additional functionality that, although not initially part of our MVP, promises to enhance user engagement. This feature allows users to input a set of waypoints, generate an optimal route, and view it directly in Google Maps.

The process of implementing the Trip Planning feature has been both challenging and rewarding. One of the major obstacles was handling location permissions. Initially, when location permissions were denied, the page would remain blank, leaving users confused. I resolved this by setting Australia as a default view, ensuring that users could input a location manually if permissions were not granted. Another complexity arose when updating the route with dynamic waypoints, as it required reworking the origin point once new waypoints were added. Through persistent troubleshooting, I was able to implement a “remove all waypoints” button, which simplified the user experience. However, one unresolved issue remains—sometimes, the API returns slightly different names from what users input, potentially causing confusion. This remains a top priority for me to fix before the final submission.

Beyond the Trip Planning feature, I also assisted with layout adjustments and contributed to design refinement, working alongside the teammate responsible for UI design.

Journey and Challenges

Developing the Trip Planning feature was a deep dive into API integration, routing algorithms, and ensuring seamless user interaction. The journey has been filled with technical challenges that required creative problem-solving, such as balancing manual input with location permissions and dynamically adjusting routes based on user inputs. These obstacles underscored the importance of flexibility and iteration when building complex features.

While the technical development was rewarding, it became apparent that I had initially underestimated the time needed for thorough testing and debugging. The task was more intricate than I had anticipated, with edge cases like permission denials and manual inputs introducing new layers of complexity. Despite these challenges, the successful implementation of core aspects, such as generating routes and linking them to Google Maps, was a rewarding experience.

The feedback we received from our tutor was instrumental in reshaping our perspective on the project. Initially, we were so focused on backend functionality and technical achievements that we overlooked the importance of user experience. The tutor emphasized that even the most advanced technologies would fail if users struggled to navigate the product intuitively. This feedback shifted our priorities, making us realize that the UI was not just a finishing touch but an integral part of the overall product. The stunning work on the UI, with a calming greenery video and smooth logo animation, showcased the importance of visual appeal in elevating the product.

Moreover, user feedback has highlighted the need for a balance between technical complexity and ease of use. During our development, particularly with features like the Trip Planning page, we faced several technical challenges, but the feedback made us aware that simplifying user interaction was just as crucial as solving these backend problems. The tutor’s advice, paired with peer insights, has encouraged us to focus on making the product more user-friendly as we approach the final delivery.

2 - ANNOTATED PROCESS PORTFOLIO

Portfolio Design

When I initially started planning my portfolio, my primary goal was to make navigation seamless and user-friendly. My initial concept involved creating separate pages for each section, which would allow for an organized and straightforward structure. I envisioned a layout where users could easily move between different parts of my work without feeling overwhelmed. The course journal, being one of the most significant sections, was originally intended to be on its own dedicated page, while other elements could reside on another. This separation was aimed at reducing clutter and focusing attention on key areas.

However, as I began designing the first introductory page, my thought process evolved. New ideas flowed in, and I started to visualize everything being on a single page. This shift meant relying heavily on JavaScript for dynamic updates, where changes or navigation between sections could occur without reloading the page. I realized that though this approach was more complex, it could offer an immersive, interactive experience that might captivate the user.

Finding the right color palette was another key aspect of my design process. I explored a wide range of websites, particularly those created by professional UI/UX designers, as they offered aesthetically pleasing inspirations. I wanted my portfolio to reflect a similar standard of visual appeal. Through this research, I carefully picked colors from various designs that resonated with me, ensuring that my portfolio had a cohesive and polished look.

Another significant design decision was about the navigation. I debated between using a traditional header or experimenting with a more innovative timeline-style navigation, where users could track their position in the portfolio as they scrolled. In the end, I opted for the traditional header but added a dynamic twist. The header now automatically updates as the user scrolls, giving a subtle nod to the timeline concept while maintaining the clarity and simplicity of traditional navigation.

Implementation

The implementation of my portfolio was an iterative process, evolving as I progressed from concept to design. I adopted a top-to-bottom approach for the development process, which allowed me to tackle each section methodically. I started by reading through my critical reflective journal, considering the feedback and reflecting on the experiences documented each week. This not only guided the content of my portfolio but also helped clarify what I wanted to convey through its design.

Once the vision for a single-page layout solidified, I realized the importance of using JavaScript to manage the various interactive elements, from scrolling effects to dynamically updating the navigation. This added layer of complexity required careful planning, especially when thinking about how users would experience the page. The balance between aesthetics and usability became my guiding principle. As I implemented different sections, I ensured that interactions were smooth and intuitive, aiming for a user-friendly experience despite the single-page complexity.

Integrating color schemes from UI/UX design inspirations helped create visual harmony. I used CSS and subtle animations to add depth to the layout, enhancing the overall experience. The reflection sections of my portfolio became a central feature, with content tailored to ensure they weren’t just informative but engaging. Through this process, I learned the importance of flexibility—how initial ideas can morph into something more complex yet rewarding.

REFLECTION

Reflecting on the journey of designing and implementing my portfolio, I realized that my thought process evolved significantly from the initial stages to the final product. Initially, I aimed for simplicity—separate pages for better navigation. However, as I dove deeper into the design, I recognized that consolidating everything into a single-page layout could enhance user engagement. This realization came from putting myself in the user’s shoes and understanding that a well-executed single page with dynamic elements could make the experience feel more cohesive and compelling.

My reflection also extends to the decisions regarding color schemes and navigation. The time I spent researching and experimenting with colors taught me how small details can make a huge difference in the overall aesthetic and user experience. The navigation debate, too, was a learning moment. The struggle between innovation and tradition was real, and settling on a balance—traditional navigation with dynamic, real-time updates—allowed me to combine usability with a creative touch.

One of the deeper reflections I had was around the iterative nature of this project. As I developed each section, I learned the value of looking back at my journal entries and using them as a foundation for the design. Reading my past reflections and analyzing the feedback I received not only helped me correct mistakes but also gave me a clearer direction moving forward. I realized how crucial it is to remain adaptable in a design process, allowing ideas to evolve while staying true to the original goals.
